# What is the Fitzpatrick Skin Type Scale?

If you have ever visited a dermatologist, been prescribed sunscreen, or undergone a laser treatment, you have likely been classified on the **Fitzpatrick Scale**. Developed in 1975 by Harvard dermatologist Dr. Thomas B. Fitzpatrick, this classification system is the global medical standard for categorizing human skin.

While originally designed for predicting UV sensitivity and sunburn risk, the Fitzpatrick Scale has been adopted by the fashion and beauty industries as a foundational framework for understanding how melanin density affects color suitability. Here is a complete breakdown.

## The Six Types

The Fitzpatrick Scale divides all human skin into six categories based on the skin's reaction to ultraviolet radiation:

### Type I — Very Fair (Always Burns, Never Tans)
Characterized by extremely pale, translucent skin with freckles, light blue or green eyes, and blonde or red hair. Very rare in India.
**Fashion Implication:** Extremely low melanin means extremely high light reflection. Must avoid overly saturated neons (which overpower) and very dark blacks (which create jarring contrast).

### Type II — Fair (Burns Easily, Tans Minimally)
Light skin that gradually tans with significant effort. Found in some Northern Indian, Kashmiri, and Northeast Indian populations.
**Fashion Implication:** Can handle moderate contrast. Jewel tones and rich pastels work beautifully. Must be cautious with extremely warm earth tones (like heavy mustard) if the undertone is cool.

### Type III — Medium Fair (Sometimes Burns, Tans Gradually)
The transitional skin type. Common in many Indian demographics. Tans reliably but can burn if exposed heavily.
**Fashion Implication:** Highly versatile. Can wear both moderate pastels and moderate jewel tones depending on undertone temperature.

### Type IV — Olive / Medium (Rarely Burns, Tans Easily)
The predominant skin type across the Indian subcontinent. Rich golden or olive undertones with easy, deep tanning capability.
**Fashion Implication:** The ultimate fashion canvas. Thrives in warm earth tones (Mustard, Terracotta, Olive) and deep jewel tones. Must avoid icy, high-white pastels that create an ashy clash.

### Type V — Brown / Dusky (Very Rarely Burns, Tans Very Easily)
Deeply pigmented skin with rich melanin density. Common across South India, Central India, and many global South Asian populations.
**Fashion Implication:** Demands high-saturation colors. Muted pastels and dusty tones disappear against the rich depth of the skin. Vibrant Emerald, Cobalt, and Bright Mustard create spectacular contrast.

### Type VI — Very Dark / Deep (Never Burns)
The deepest melanin concentration. Incredibly rich, deep brown to near-black skin.
**Fashion Implication:** The most powerful canvas for bold, bright fashion. Crisp White, Electric Blue, and vivid Magenta create breathtaking contrast that lighter skin types simply cannot replicate.

## Beyond Fitzpatrick: The Undertone Layer

The Fitzpatrick Scale measures melanin quantity (how dark or light) but does not measure melanin quality (the warm/cool temperature). Two Type IV individuals can have radically different undertones—one deeply golden (Warm) and one distinctly olive-gray (Cool). Their fashion palettes will be completely different despite sharing the same Fitzpatrick number.
